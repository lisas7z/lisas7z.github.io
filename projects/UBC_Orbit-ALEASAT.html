Project Background

UBC Orbit is a satellite design team comprising over 100 undergraduate and graduate students from a variety of disciplines. ALEASAT is a 1U (10cm x 10cm x 10cm) LEO (low Earth orbit) observation satellite that can be used by radio amateurs in disaster monitor and relief opeartions through image transmission directly from the satellite to the ground station. ALEASAT is currently being developed by student members of UBC Orbit and SFU Satellite Design Team, and the project is supported by UBC, the Radio Amateurs of Canada, and the European Space Agency's Fly Your Satellite! Programme (ESA). The project is still in the process of building and testing with an aim to launch in a few years.

Mechanical Simulation

(Disclaimer: Only methodology and basic information about the satellite will be presented here.)

  1. Structural Analysis Approach

    2. The primary objectives of the structural analysis is to ensure the mechanical integrity and the peformance of the satellite under various loads and environmental conditions it will experience during launch, deloyment, and operation in space. Specifically, the structural mechanical simulation utilizes ANSYS Mechanical to verify whether or not the design meets the following critical criterion:

    1. the Margin of Safety (MoS) for all materials must be above zero;
    2. the resonance frequency must be above cetain level, the specific value varies depending on the Launch Provider;
    3. evaluate the deformation of the satellite under quasi-static loading in all directions;
    4. evaluate the performance of the satellite under stochastic vibrational loading in all directions during launch.

  2. FEM (Finite Element Model)

    3. Figure 1: ALEASAT simplified model.

    To achieve the objectives, the analysis approach involves performing mechanical simulation to a finite element model (FEM) of the satellite structure, capturing the essential features and behaviours of the full-scale structure while reducing the computational complexity.

    The FEM is first generated through simplifications, including:

    1. neglecting electronic chips with minimal mass contribution on PCBs;
    2. representing PCBs as shell elements, capturing in-plane and bending deformations;
    3. representing heavy components with minimal structural deformation as point masses, preserving inertial effects and mass distribution;
    4. representing fasteners as stiff spring elements with defined preload forces, simulating clamping effects;

    then, the simplified model is discretized into smaller and manageable elements for numerical analysis through meshing.

    Note that preload forces are calculated by $$T = KDP$$ where

    • T = tightening torque;
    • K = torque coefficient (0.3 for dry threads);
    • D = nominal diameter;
    • P = bolt preload.

    In order to generate high-quality meshes resulting in higher simulation accuracy, the following key steps need to be considered:

    1. partition the model into regions based on geometries, and apply different mesh types in each region;
    2. incorporate adaptive mesh refinement by setting coarser mesh in less critical regions and higher element density in regions with higher stress concentrations or contact surfaces;
    3. examine mesh convergence by starting with a coarser mesh and refine it progressively, ensuring results do not change significantly with finer meshes.

  3. Modal Analysis

    4. Modal analysis is a numerical technique used to determine the natural frequencies and mode shapes of the satellite structure. It helps ensuring the structural robustness against dynamic vibrational loading by checking that resonance does not occur.

    The first five modes and their natural frequencies is a sufficient representative of the overall system, and a critical frequency is given by ESA, requiring the first natural frequency must not be less than the critical frequency. Vibration modes with modal effective mass greater than 10% of the total mass are reported as critical modes, which might need to be carefully analyzed and controlled in the future.

    Deformations resulted from modal analysis for each mode can be viewed in ANSYS.

    Figure 2: ALEASAT modal analysis mode 2 result, with components hidden for result visibility.

  4. Quasi-static Analysis

    5. Quasi-static analysis is a type of structural analysis that evalutes the system's response to gradually applied loads. This analysis assumes that the structure reaches equilibrium at each step of the loading process. In our simulation, a 20g acceleration will be applied in each principal direction, simulating static launch loads to ensure the satellite can withstand gravitational forces and vibrations.

    We need to apply boundary conditions that replicate the constraints within a deployment launcher (e.g., a P-POD):

    1. Fixed supports should be applied in the ±Z directions to account for the presence of multiple satellites inside the launcher;
    2. Small displacements should be allowed in the ±X and ±Y directions to provide tolerances and prevent damage to the satellite during the launch process.

    By evaluating the deformation and equivalent stress in ANSYS in each direction, MOS can be calculated by: $$MoS = \frac{Strength}{SF \times Stress} - 1$$ where Stress and Safety Factor (SF) will be different for each material.

    Figure 3: ALEASAT quasi-static analysis equivalent stress in X direction.

  5. Random Vibration Analysis

    6. Random vibration analysis is a method used to evaluate the structure's response to non-deterministic dynamic (unpredictable) loads. It ensures

    1. the CubeSat can survive launch vibrations;
    2. electronic components will not fail due to mechanical fatigue;
    3. bolted joints remain secure under dynamic loading.

    The results from the modal analysis are used as input for the random vibration analysis. According to the guidelines provided in General environmental Verification Standard (GEVS) For GSFC Flight Programs and Projects , random vibration needs to be performed to the qualification level with specific test profile listed in Table 2.4-3. Maximum deformation and maximum stress in each direction will be evaluated with 98% confidence interval (3σ); MoS will be calculated for each axis, and displacement results will be obtained to verify that the satellite structure stays within the required dynamic envelope.
